Introduction

Impressionism, an art movement that emerged in the late 19th century, brought a revolutionary approach to painting. Artists of this movement sought to capture fleeting moments, emphasizing light, color, and everyday scenes rather than the formal subjects that were traditionally favored. Key figures like Claude Monet, Pierre-Auguste Renoir, and Edgar Degas broke away from conventional techniques, using quick brushstrokes and vivid colors to portray the world in a new, refreshing way. Impressionism was not only about aesthetics; it challenged established art norms and inspired future movements, setting the stage for modern art.

Famous Artists

Claude Monet, Pierre-Auguste Renoir, and Edgar Degas were among the pioneers of Impressionism. Their unique styles and approaches to light and color helped define the movement and inspired countless others.

Core Techniques

Impressionists are known for their rapid brushstrokes, use of vibrant colors, and focus on natural light. This technique allowed them to depict scenes as though captured in a moment, with textures and shades that mimic reality.

Influence on Modern Art

Impressionism was a precursor to many modern art movements. By challenging traditional methods and focusing on everyday life, it paved the way for movements like Expressionism and Fauvism in the 20th century.
